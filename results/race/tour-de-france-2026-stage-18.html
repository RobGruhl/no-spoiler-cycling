§ 01 · The stage

Carapaz flies solo at Orcières as UAE illness crisis deepens ahead of Alpe d'Huez.

Stage 18 covered 185.2km from Voiron into the Hautes-Alpes, opening the Alpine trilogy with five categorised climbs and a summit finish at the Orcières-Merlette ski station (1,850m). From the opening kilometres, a fierce battle raged for the breakaway, with Tadej Pogačar's powerful positioning on the Côte d'Engins (1st category) preventing large groups escaping early.

A 14-rider leading group finally consolidated around the 70km mark, including Richard Carapaz, Matteo Jorgenson, Mads Pedersen, Mauro Schmid, Valentin Paret-Peintre, Tobias Johannessen and Raúl García Pierna. Pedersen, seizing the chance to extend his points lead, collected the maximum 25 points at the intermediate sprint 56km from the finish — pushing his lead over Jasper Philipsen from 7 to 32 points. He would later lose contact with the front group as the road tilted upward.

With 45km remaining, the leading group splintered. Six riders entered the final 7km climb to Orcières-Merlette with several minutes over the peloton: Carapaz, Jorgenson, Schmid, Paret-Peintre, Johannessen and García Pierna. At 4km to go Carapaz launched a first probing attack; the pace eased and he recovered contact. At 3.2km to go he attacked again — explosively — and this time the gap held and grew. Paret-Peintre cracked immediately; Jorgenson and Schmid chased hard but crossed the line 45 seconds behind.

The GC peloton arrived 4m 30s down with no time differences — standings unchanged heading into back-to-back Alpe d'Huez summit finishes (Stages 19 and 20). The day's deeper story belonged to UAE Team Emirates-XRG, swept by a virus since Monday's rest day: Brandon McNulty abandoned with 21km remaining; Tim Wellens and Florian Vermeersch struggled visibly; Adam Yates, already compromised from Stage 17, was limited throughout. Pogačar finished safely in yellow but enters the Alpine weekend short-handed.

§ 04 · Rider-by-rider · tracked riders

Storylines from the stage

🏳️ Richard Carapaz EF Education - EasyPost
STAGE HUNTER P1 winner

Carapaz claimed his second Tour de France stage win (after Superdévoluy in 2024) and 25th professional victory overall with a characteristic gutsy solo effort. He was marked as a danger from the gun but allowed into the breakaway given his remote GC position. On the final climb he attacked twice in quick succession — the second move, at 3.2km, created a gap that Jorgenson and Schmid could not close. The win also vaulted him into the top 10 overall at +21:00 and sharpened the KOM battle: Carapaz sits third in the polka-dot standings just 7 points off the lead.

🏳️ Matteo Jorgenson Team Visma | Lease a Bike
STAGE HUNTER P3 +0:45

Jorgenson rode an excellent stage, helping shepherd the six-man front group and chasing hard after Carapaz's decisive attack. He could not match the Ecuadorian's acceleration and finished level with Schmid at 45 seconds, taking third on a summit finish that suits his profile. The result is a strong confidence marker for Visma ahead of the Alpe d'Huez stages.

🇩🇪 Mads Pedersen Lidl - Trek
STAGE HUNTER — s.t. (peloton group)

Pedersen executed a calculated mission — getting into the early breakaway specifically to contest the intermediate sprint. He secured all 25 available points at the sprint 56km from the finish, extending his green jersey lead over Philipsen from 7 to 32 points. With only one genuine sprint stage remaining (Stage 21, Paris), this move likely settles the points classification. Pedersen dropped from the front group as the climbing intensified and finished further back.

🇧🇪 Jasper Philipsen Alpecin-Premier Tech
LEADER — +4:30 (GC group)

Philipsen finished safely in the peloton and was unable to join the breakaway — the climbing terrain did not suit him. His green jersey lead over Pedersen was cut from 7 to a 32-point deficit, which now makes Philipsen the underdog in the points fight with only the Paris stage remaining as a realistic sprint opportunity.

🇸🇮 Tadej Pogačar UAE Team Emirates - XRG
LEADER — +4:30 (GC group, yellow jersey retained)

Pogačar finished safely with the GC group, retaining yellow with a 4:32 lead over Evenepoel intact. The stage revealed his team's vulnerability: Brandon McNulty abandoned with illness mid-stage; Tim Wellens and Florian Vermeersch both showed signs of sickness. Despite his substantial overall lead, Pogačar heads into back-to-back Alpe d'Huez mountain top finishes with a depleted support roster. He remained upbeat: 'I think we can manage. It's the third week, all the teams are suffering.'

🇧🇪 Remco Evenepoel Red Bull - BORA - hansgrohe
LEADER — +4:32 (GC, 2nd overall)

Evenepoel rode safely in the GC group, maintaining second overall at 4:32 behind Pogačar. With UAE losing domestiques to illness ahead of two Alpe d'Huez stages, Evenepoel's team is watching the opportunity closely — though a 4:32 gap on back-to-back summit finishes remains a steep target.

🏳️ Adam Yates UAE Team Emirates - XRG
DOMESTIQUE INCIDENT finished (GC group or behind)

After losing 26 minutes to stomach illness on Stage 17, Yates continued in Stage 18 but with limited capacity. Pogačar's post-stage comments suggested Yates was 'getting better', but his ability to support in two high-altitude summit finishes remains a critical unknown for UAE's Alpine strategy.

⚠ Continued racing after Stage 17 stomach illness — fitness uncertain for Alpine stages
🇬🇧 Tom Pidcock Pinarello Q36.5 Pro Cycling Team
LEADER — +4:30 (GC group, 8th overall at +12:58)

Pidcock rode in the GC group and finished without incident, maintaining 8th overall. With two Alpe d'Huez stages ahead, the summit finish specialist will be aiming for a top-5 overall move — his form heading into the Alpine weekend is the key unknown.
